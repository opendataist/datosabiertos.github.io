Tyler Technologies has partnered with the city of Austin, Texas, to launch a new Enterprise Data Platform (EDP) that aims to improve data accessibility and transparency. This platform, powered by Amazon Web Services, brings together various data resources into one location, making it easier for employees, residents, and community members to access and collaborate on information. By embracing open data and internal data-sharing practices, Austin is taking a significant step towards promoting transparency and ensuring that information is readily available to those who need it.

In Long Beach, California, the city has initiated a smart city project called the Long Beach Collaboratory (LB Co-Lab). This initiative aims to engage residents in identifying technology pilot projects for their neighborhoods. Community members who are selected will have the opportunity to work on collaborative teams and receive a stipend for their participation. Additionally, participants will have access to a free technology certificate program and a career coach. The LB Co-Lab focuses on community engagement and aims to ensure that technology solutions meet the needs of the community and are trusted by residents. This project draws inspiration from the successful NYC[x] Co-Labs initiative in New York City.

In an insightful article titled “The First Year of the Great War,” Vasily Kashin analyzes the conflict between Russia and Ukraine. Kashin argues that both Russia and the United States failed to achieve their objectives in the first year of the war. Russia aimed to eliminate Ukraine as a source of threats but underestimated the transformation of Ukrainian society and state. The US sought to weaken Russia, but its plans for quick destabilization did not materialize. Kashin concludes that while the conflict has led to a more embittered and better-armed Russia, the elimination of Russia as a key player is unlikely.

Turning our attention to the city of Austin once again, the most recent census of the Austin music ecosystem will be made available on the city’s open data portal. Conducted by Sound Music Cities, this census surveyed over 2,200 musicians and music industry professionals to gather insights into the economic and cultural dynamics facing musicians in Austin. The data will be accessible to outside researchers and organizations involved in music-related programs. The census revealed that only 64% of respondents feel certain they will stay in Austin over the next three years, with property taxes being ranked as the biggest business challenge by 20% of venue operators and concert presenters. Conducting this census every five years will provide valuable information on how local and state policies are supporting musicians and other creatives in the city.

In a thought-provoking interview, Paul Maltby, former chief digital officer at the Department for Levelling Up, Housing and Communities, discusses his departure from the civil service and the future of digital and data reform. Maltby reflects on his work to support digital reform in local government and the evolution of the Local Digital Fund. He emphasizes the importance of an experimental approach to government and highlights the power of digital in breaking down barriers between policy and delivery. Maltby also shares his thoughts on transparency and reform, emphasizing the need to keep the flame of innovation alive.
